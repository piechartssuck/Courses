Read

  • Chapters 8-9 in James McDavid, Program Evaluation and Performance Measurement. While not required Chapter 7 is on economic evaluation, that is the content is solely focused on assessing whether the potential or actual benefits from interventions are greater than the projected or existing loss in benefits from a reduction in other programs. If the idea of something like a cost-benefit analysis interests you, please read this.

  • Chapters 6-7 in Tony Albano, Introduction to Educational and Psychological Measurement Using R

Note: Firefox appears to have some problems rendering the formulas in the text properly. Google Chrome, Safari, and Tor do not appear to have this problem. With that said, Chapter 7 is somewhat dense and you are not expected to memorize formulas. Get the basic idea of what an IRT is and what it is used for and ask questions as needed!

Suggestions

Next week we cover Item Response Theory (IRT) in greater detail. While Chapter 7 in Albano’s online text is a needed read, it is a bit difficult to fully comprehend at first so we’ll be emphasizing the procedural aspects of conducting one. If you want a grounded conceptual (not statistical or mathematical) introduction, check out Karon F. Cook’s short 6-part series. She does a really good job bringing a relatively high level concept down to Earth.

Questions to reflect on

This week first head over to the new version and old version of Spurious Correlations: the often hilarious and always ridiculous examples of why correlation doesn’t imply causation. Pick one out, paste it into a word document and then write a one paragraph narrative on

  • why you chose the one that you did?
  • do you think correlation is ever enough? what is that point?
  • how do you think correlation is tied to measurement?

Stuck? Please take a look at this read of some common survey design mistakes that lead to all kinds of measurement errors.
