The Final Proposal

There are three items to submit this week

  1. Elevator Pitch. Create a 30 second to 2 minute elevator pitch introducing yourself, describing the evaluation, research, or project that your instrument would be used for, and its significance1. If you use a third-party service (e.g. YouTube), please provide the link.

  2. Abstract. In a one to two page double spaced 12 point narrative (~ 300 word count), describe your research and its significance, the proposed instrument, why it is needed, and the results of your validity and reliability testing. Furthermore if amendments would be needed, please note those as well. Consider this as a supplement of your elevator pitch where you can expand on what was said.

  3. Instrument. Fill in the four column table shown below. Be sure to use the factor analysis you conducted last week to denote or amend your grouping variables. Please remember to interpret them into actual categories.

Please submit the

  • Elevator Pitch and Abstract to #elevator-pitch in a single posting on Slack.

  • Elevator Pitch, Abstract, and a copy of your Instrument to eCampus.

You can review the entire sequence leading up to the final proposal can be found on the main Tasks page.

R Measurement EDA

Firstly please download the following de-identified data set taken from a larger instrument that was used in a recent program evaluation I led.


After unzipping it, you will find a data set, codebook, and file that ends in .Rmd. The latter is called a Rmarkdown file which is basically the Microsoft Work of the R world without all of the annoyances. Exactly like before, throw everything into a single folder, open up the .Rmd file, and set the working directory. This document serves as a reporting template and directions are included within the body. You may use any source other than another human being, especially since the walkthroughs contain the steps needed to complete this task.

You will need to knit this document as a pdf and submit it to eCampus. If you think I’ve confused R with a ball of yarn, that hasn’t happened yet. In the meantime, knitting a .Rmd file is similar to running a .R script. To get a visual take on this, there is also a brief introduction on Rmarkdown on the Resources page that briefly goes over this. A basic overview and benefits of Rmarkdown can be found in the text R for Data Science.
